Part 1 · Foundations

What is Database Activity Monitoring?

Database Activity Monitoring (DAM) is a security practice — and a category of tools — that watches everything happening inside your databases in real time: who connected, what queries they ran, what data they read or changed, and whether any of it looks risky.

Think of your database as a bank vault holding your most valuable asset: data — customer records, payment details, health information, intellectual property. A firewall guards the building's front door, but once someone is inside, who is watching what they actually do with the vault? That's DAM. It's the CCTV + alarm system for your data.

👁️

Visibility

A complete record of every login, query, and data access across all your databases.

🚨

Detection

Automatic alerts when someone does something unusual or against the rules.

🛡️

Response

The ability to block, kill, or quarantine a risky session before damage is done.

📋

Compliance

Tamper-proof audit trails that prove to regulators you're watching your data.

In one sentence: DAM answers the question "Who is doing what to my data, right now — and is any of it dangerous?"
Part 1 · Foundations

Why does DAM matter?

1. The biggest threats are often inside the network

Traditional security (firewalls, VPNs) protects the perimeter. But data breaches frequently come from credentials that are already trusted: a compromised app account, a curious employee, a departing DBA copying the customer list, or an attacker who stole a password. To them the database says "come in." DAM is what notices the bulk download of 2 million customer records at 3 a.m. — because behaviour, not just identity, is being watched.

2. Compliance requires it

Most data-protection regulations legally require you to monitor and log access to sensitive data:

RegulationProtectsWhat it expects of you
GDPREU personal dataTrack access to personal data; support data-subject requests; report breaches fast.
PCI-DSSCard / payment dataLog all access to cardholder data; monitor privileged users.
HIPAAUS health recordsAudit who viewed patient data and when.
SOXFinancial reportingProve the integrity and controls around financial data.

3. When something goes wrong, you need the story

After an incident, the first questions are: What was accessed? By whom? How much? When? Without DAM you're guessing. With it, you have a precise, time-stamped, tamper-evident record — the difference between "we contained it" and "we have no idea."

The stakes: the average data breach costs millions and takes months to even detect. DAM's whole purpose is to collapse that detection time from months to minutes.

Key terms you'll meet

You don't need to memorise these — they'll click as you go — but here's a friendly map. (Full glossary at the end.)

Principal
The "who" — a user or service account that connects to a database (e.g. app_crm, dba_mueller).
Query
A command sent to the database. SELECT reads data; INSERT/UPDATE/DELETE change it; GRANT changes permissions.
Session
One connection from a principal to a database, from login to logout — the container for all their queries.
Sensitive data / PII
Data that must be protected — names, emails, card numbers, tax IDs, health records. PII = Personally Identifiable Information.
Policy / Rule
A condition that defines "risky." E.g. "alert if anyone reads more than 10,000 rows of PII."
Alert
What's raised when activity matches a policy — the thing a security analyst investigates.
Quarantine
The response: isolating or blocking a risky account/session so it can't do (more) harm.
Agent
The lightweight software that actually captures the database activity and feeds it to TooVix.

Part 2 · How DAM works

Stage 1 — Capturing activity

Before you can monitor anything, you have to see it. DAM tools capture database activity in a few ways; TooVix supports the main ones:

MethodHow it worksTrade-off
Agent-basedA small agent runs near the database and reads its activity stream.Rich detail; needs deployment.
Inline proxyTraffic flows through TooVix, so it can block in real time, not just watch.Enables active defense; sits in the path.
Network / logsReads network traffic or the database's own audit logs.Low touch; less granular.

The choice of method is called the capture mode, and how widely you've deployed it is your coverage. More coverage = fewer blind spots.

Beginner takeaway: a database TooVix doesn't capture is a database it can't protect. Step one is always getting monitoring onto your data sources.

Capture modes & coverage — in depth

The section above introduced capture at a high level. In practice TooVix offers four capture modes, and each one watches a different path into your database with different powers. You rarely pick just one — you combine them to close blind spots. Here's the fuller picture.

At a glance — the four capture modes

Where each mode sits, and what it can do.

Dashed lines = telemetry each mode sends outbound to the DAM (agents dial out; the control plane never connects into your DB network — which is why this works for private, no-public-IP databases). Only the inline proxy sits in the traffic path, so it's the only mode that can block in real time.

①

Network agent · installed · passive

Sniffs the database's network traffic (libpcap, protocol decode). No path change, ~0 overhead, and it sees every networked connection — including ones that bypass a proxy. Blind to local/IPC; cannot block.

②

Host agent (eBPF) · installed · passive

Runs on the DB host kernel. Sees every connection reaching the DB process — including local sockets, shared memory and IPC nothing else can. Deepest visibility; limited local enforcement.

③

Inline proxy · installed · inline

A gateway in the data path — clients connect through it. The only mode that can block / quarantine, and the only one that sees the real end-user behind a pooled connection. Only sees traffic routed through it.

④

Agentless (Pull / Push) · no install

Reads native DB audit logs / the DB profiler, or consumes cloud audit streams (Cloud Push). Works for managed / PaaS databases where you can't (or don't want to) install on the host. After-the-fact; cannot block.

Mental model: the inline proxy is a gate the traffic passes through (so it can stop it); the network and host agents are cameras pointed at the database (they see traffic arrive but can't stop it). Each catches a path the others can't — so combining them closes blind spots.

Part 2 · How DAM works

Stage 2 & 3 — Analyse and detect with policies

Raw activity is just noise until you decide what counts as risky. That's the job of policies (also called rules). A policy is a plain-language condition, for example:

  • "Bulk read of sensitive data" — someone reads an unusually large number of PII rows.
  • "Privileged off-hours access" — a DBA touches personal data at 3 a.m.
  • "Credential brute force" — many failed logins from one source.
  • "GRANT of DBA / SYSDBA" — someone hands out top-level privileges.

Enabled vs Monitor vs Disabled

A crucial concept for beginners — every policy has a state:

StateWhat happensWhen to use
EnabledMatches raise real alerts.You trust the rule.
Monitor (shadow)Matches are counted but no alert fires.Testing a new rule to see if it's noisy before it wakes anyone up.
DisabledThe rule never runs.Not relevant to you.
Best practice: put a brand-new rule in Monitor first. Watch it for a few days. If it only fires on genuinely bad things, promote it to Enabled. This is how you avoid "alert fatigue."
Part 2 · How DAM works

Stage 4 — Alerts and severity

When enabled-policy conditions are met, DAM raises an alert. Not every alert is equally urgent, so each carries a severity that tells your team what to look at first:

● Critical ● High ● Medium ● Low

A healthy workflow treats alerts like a queue: acknowledge that you've seen it, investigate what happened, then resolve it (or escalate/quarantine if it's real). Alerts you decide are harmless can be marked a false positive, which teaches the system to stop bugging you about that pattern.

A false positive is an alert that turned out to be benign. Tuning them down is a normal, healthy part of running DAM — not a failure.
Part 2 · How DAM works

Stage 5 — Responding to threats

Detection without response is just a very good alarm nobody acts on. When an alert is a genuine threat, DAM lets you act:

⛔

Quarantine an account

Block a principal so it can't open new sessions.

✂️

Terminate a session

Kill the live connection immediately, mid-query.

↗️

Escalate

Hand it to on-call / senior responders.

🎯

Active defense

Plant decoys (fake data) to catch and study attackers.

Quarantine has two flavours you'll use often: Terminate ends the live session but keeps the account blocked, while Release lifts a quarantine once you've confirmed things are fine.

Knowing what to protect — classification & compliance

You can't prioritise protecting data if you don't know where the sensitive stuff lives. Classification scans your databases and labels columns that hold PII — emails, card numbers, tax IDs — so policies and reports can focus on what actually matters.

Related tools you'll see in TooVix:

  • Masking — hide or scramble sensitive values (e.g. show ****1234 instead of a full card number) so people can work without seeing raw PII.
  • Access Governance — control who is allowed to reach which data, including just-in-time (JIT) access that's granted temporarily and auto-expires.
  • Compliance Center, DSAR & Audit Trail — prove you're doing all of the above: posture against GDPR/PCI/HIPAA/SOX, handle "what data do you hold on me?" requests, and keep an immutable log.
The Audit Trail is deliberately tamper-evident — even admins can't quietly rewrite history. That's what makes it trustworthy to a regulator.

Part 3 · Using TooVix DAM

Your first day: the quick-start path

Here's the shortest sensible path from empty to protected. Each step below has its own detailed section.

  1. Register a database so TooVix knows about your data source.
  2. Deploy monitoring to start capturing its activity.
  3. Classify to discover where your sensitive data is.
  4. Tune policies so the right things raise alerts.
  5. Triage alerts as they come in.
  6. Quarantine anything genuinely malicious.
  7. Report to prove compliance.
Button labels below in purple are the exact text you'll click in the app. Grey chips are secondary buttons. If a screen looks slightly different, you may be on a newer build — the sidebar names are your reliable anchor.
Step 1

Register your first database

Go to Databases in the sidebar.

  1. Click Register instance at the top right. (An "instance" is a database server — it can hold several databases.)
  2. Choose how to add it:
    • Cloud Discovery — TooVix auto-finds databases in a connected cloud account.
    • Manual — you type the host, port and engine (e.g. PostgreSQL, MySQL, SQL Server, Oracle) yourself.
  3. Complete the form and confirm. Your instance now appears in the list.
  4. To add another database under an existing instance, click ＋ DB on that instance's row.
Prefer to let TooVix find things for you? Use Discovery → Run scan, then Approve and Register each database it finds. View registered jumps back to the Databases list.
Step 2

Deploy monitoring

A registered database isn't watched until an agent is monitoring it.

  1. From Databases, click Deploy monitoring on the instance — or go to Agents & Coverage and click Deploy monitoring.
  2. Follow the deploy steps for your environment. The agent begins capturing activity.
  3. Check Agents & Coverage to confirm the agent is healthy (use Refresh to reload). To remove one, click Remove.
  4. Review how activity is captured under Capture Modes.
Once an agent is live, activity starts flowing into the pipeline you learned about in Part 2 — you'll see it show up on the Dashboard and, if anything's risky, in Alerts.
Step 3

Classify your sensitive data

Now find where the PII lives. Go to Classification.

  1. Click Run Scan. TooVix inspects columns and labels sensitive ones (emails, card numbers, tax IDs, etc.).
  2. Review the results. Sensitive columns now carry tags that policies and reports can target.
  3. Want to hide sensitive values from certain users? Go to Masking and click Add to create a masking rule for a database.
Classification is what makes a rule like "bulk read of sensitive data" meaningful — the system now knows which data is sensitive.
Step 4

Tune your detection policies

Go to Policies & Rules. New workspaces start with a set of sensible default rules, so you're not beginning from scratch.

  1. Review the list. Each rule shows its current state.
  2. Set a rule's state:
    • ✓ Enable — raise real alerts on matches.
    • ◎ Move to Monitor — shadow mode: count matches silently while you evaluate the rule.
    • ○ Disable — turn the rule off.
  3. Create your own rule with New rule.
  4. Need to whitelist a known-good pattern? Use Add exception; remove one later with Revoke. Toggle Active / All to include revoked/expired items. Every "false positive" you mark on an alert also shows up here as a suppression — so this is where you revoke it if you want the rule to fire again.
Remember the golden rule from Part 2: new rules start in Monitor, graduate to Enable once proven quiet.
Step 5

Triage alerts

This is the day-to-day heartbeat of DAM. Go to Alerts.

  1. Work top-down by severity (Critical first). Click an alert to see the who / what / where / when.
  2. Move it through its lifecycle:
    • ✓ Acknowledge — "I've seen this, I'm on it." (✓ Acknowledge all clears a batch.)
    • ✓ Resolve — investigated and closed.
    • ↗ Escalate — hand to on-call.
    • ⎚ Session timeline — reconstruct the full session behind the alert.
  3. If it's a genuine threat, hit ⛔ Quarantine & kill to block the account and kill its live session immediately.
  4. If it's harmless, click ✗ False positive, then Confirm false positive to tune it out. This creates a suppression so that rule stops firing for the scope you chose (this principal, this object, both, or the whole rule).
Changed your mind about a false positive? You can revoke it. Go to Policies & Rules → the Exceptions tab, find the suppression, and click Revoke — the rule starts firing again for that scope. Revoked suppressions are kept in the trail (toggle All to see revoked/expired ones), so there's a full history of what was suppressed and when.
Use ⤓ Export to pull alerts for a report or ticket, and Refresh to fetch the latest.
Step 6

Quarantine risky accounts

The Quarantine screen is your containment console — every held or blocked session and account lives here.

  1. To proactively block a principal, click ⛔ Quarantine account.
  2. Set default containment behaviour with ⛨ Quarantine policy.
  3. On a held session you have two choices:
    • Terminate — kill the live session and keep the account blocked.
    • Release — lift the quarantine; the principal may reconnect.
Terminate vs Release: Terminate = "stop this now, stay blocked." Release = "all clear, let them back in." When in doubt, Terminate and investigate — you can always Release afterwards.
Step 7

Compliance & reports

Turn all that monitoring into proof.

  • Compliance Center — see your posture against GDPR / PCI-DSS / HIPAA / SOX. Click Generate report or grab an Evidence pack; Mask a flagged column right from here.
  • DSAR Manager — handle "what data do you hold on me?" requests: New request to log one, Open to work it, Download evidence.
  • Audit Trail — the immutable, filterable log of everything. Read-only; use the filters to search.
  • Reports — Generate a report, build one with Custom report, set recurring runs with Schedule, and export via Export CSV or Print / PDF.
Step 8

Meet the AI Copilot

Open Copilot (or the ✦ Ask TooVix AI button in the top bar). It has two tabs:

Grounded in your live environment. Ask "What are my top risks right now?" or "Summarise today's critical alerts."

An admin first connects an AI account (Anthropic Claude or OpenAI) via the Copilot ⚙ Configure button. After that, anyone in the workspace can chat.

The Help tab is scoped on purpose: it helps with TooVix and database-security topics and politely declines unrelated questions.

Part 4 · Wrap-up

Best practices for beginners

  • Cover everything. Every unmonitored database is a blind spot — register and deploy widely.
  • Classify early. You can't protect sensitive data you haven't located.
  • Shadow, then enable. New policies go to Monitor first to avoid alert fatigue.
  • Work the queue daily. Acknowledge → investigate → resolve. Don't let alerts pile up.
  • Tune false positives. Every one you dismiss makes the system sharper.
  • Contain fast, ask later. For a real threat, Quarantine & kill first — you can Release if it was fine.
  • Report regularly. Scheduled reports keep auditors (and your future self) happy.
  • Least privilege. Give people the narrowest role that lets them do their job.
